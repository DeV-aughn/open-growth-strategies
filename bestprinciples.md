01
The one thing
The winners have all made the same trade.
Every agency in the top five has traded generality for a specific promise, made to a specific kind of contractor, backed by a specific number. Every agency in the bottom five is still hedging.

That's the entire finding. What follows is the evidence for it — page by page, quote by quote, offer by offer — and a design brief that translates those observations into a site you could build next month.

The mid-market trades-marketing category is unusually good for a new entrant because most incumbents have gotten fat and lazy. Ten of the twenty-eight agencies surveyed lead with the phrase "digital marketing" or "marketing agency" in their hero — a category name masquerading as a promise. Fifteen refuse to publish any pricing. Twenty publish no case study with a dollar figure attached to a real client name. The five who win the category are the five who broke each of those defaults.

The hero is warmer than most — "join" rather than "convert," "satisfied contractors" rather than "clients." That decision matters. Because nobody else does this, Hook harvests every "hvac marketing pricing" long-tail query in the category — the pricing page is itself their #1 SEO asset. It also functions as a self-qualifier: anyone who blanches at the numbers exits before the call.

What their marketing engine actually is

The unbundled service-menu offer (SEO + PPC + Web as separate SKUs is 2015 agency-shape; productize it). The explicit $2M+ revenue gate leaves the entire $500k–$2M segment on the table.

05
Ten patterns
The differences that consistently separate top from bottom.
Ten dimensions. Same observation on each — the top five picked a side, the bottom five stayed in the middle. Middle is where trades-agency websites go to die.

Positioning
Top five
Headlines are quantified outcomes (50–100 booked jobs), system frames (Consistent Demand · Predictable Revenue), emotional benefit stacks (More Jobs · More Revenue · Less Stress), or diagnostic reframes (presence problem, not lead problem).
Bottom five
Headlines are category names dressed as promises ("Marketing for Home Services + More," "We specialize in website design for contractors"). Zero differentiation embedded in the actual words.
Proof density
Top five
Aggregate numbers above the fold ($240M driven, $500M created), then named case studies with client + revenue delta, then video testimonials. Multiple layers of evidence within the first two screens.
Bottom five
Round-number claims without context ("thousands of clients") or oddly-precise claims that read as fake ("972,109+ leads"). No named-dollar case studies anywhere.
Offer construction
Top five
Two dominant models — transparent tiered pricing (Hook) or productized subscription framing (Comrade's Revenue Engine, Rival's "systems"). Both remove sales friction and pre-qualify the buyer.
Bottom five
Universal "Request a Quote" model. Every path leads to a sales call. No self-serve qualification, no productization, no way to filter yourself in or out before the meeting.
Site structure
Top five
Vertical × service landing pages (Hook has separate /hvac-marketing/website-design/, /plumbing-marketing/website-design/, etc.). Case studies as first-class content with named-outcome titles.
Bottom five
One-size-fits-all service pages. Verticals listed as bullet points or footer links rather than dedicated pages. Case studies buried or missing entirely.
Visual language
Top five
Distinctive palettes with an anchor color (Hook = neon yellow on gray; KickCharge = truck-wrap-forward). Real photography of real teams and real clients.
Bottom five
Generic blue/white/gray with stock imagery of hard hats and blueprints. Visually interchangeable — this is the diagnostic. If you can swap logos and nothing else changes, the design is failing.
Copy voice
Top five
Plain-spoken, benefit-framed, contractor-native language — "full schedules," "revenue that compounds," "booked jobs." "You" outweighs "we." Reads like it was written by someone who has been on a contractor's phone.
Bottom five
Feature-listy, "results-driven," "digital marketing," industry jargon that could apply to any B2B category. Reads like it was written by an agency about itself.
Conversion mechanics
Top five
Two primary CTAs on hero (Hook: "Book An Intro Call" + "View Contractor Results"). Named human account managers with photos. Case study CTAs interspersed through content rather than one form at the bottom.
Bottom five
Single generic CTA ("Get a Quote," "Contact Us"). Long forms. No calendar embeds. No proof of what happens after the click.
Trust architecture
Top five
Awards and certifications shown as a strip (Google Premier, ServiceTitan, Inc. 5000), then peer-network or named client logos, then case study numbers. Trust is layered across the page, not concentrated in a footer badge.
Bottom five
BBB badge, Google Partner badge, "trusted since 2004" claim. Flat trust — one strip in the footer or hero, then nothing.
Founder presence
Top five
Founder is visible by name and often by book or podcast. Hook's Tim Brown has a Golden Rule pledge with signature. KickCharge's Antonelli wrote the book. Rival's Eric Thomas wrote the book. Comrade shows Ivan Vislavskiy's photo.
Bottom five
Founder absent. Team page (if it exists) is stock-corporate. The agency is a faceless entity, which is fatal in a category where buyers are choosing a partner, not a vendor.
Speed & polish
Top five
All feel fast and modern — React/Next.js-era builds, smooth transitions, no visible jank. The site itself functions as demonstrable proof that the agency can build a good site.
Bottom five
Footbridge and Townsquare feel like WordPress-with-theme builds from 2020. Perceptible load lag on two of five. If your own site loads slowly, no case study will convince a buyer you'd build them a fast one.

Your design brief
Twelve principles for the site you should build.
Each principle is derived directly from what the top five do that the bottom five don't. Each one closes with a specific application for your NC-based, high-margin service niche play.

01
Lead the hero with a quantified transformation, not a category name.
Contractor Marketing Pros wins on this axis with "Get 50–100 Booked Jobs Per Month." Rival wins by naming cash-flow states. Both convert to revenue math in the buyer's head in under two seconds. Every bottom-five hero fails this test because they name a category the buyer already knows they're shopping for.
"Add 20–40 booked pool routes a month to your service map." Or "Turn one waste-management contract into three." Pick the transformation that ends in the buyer counting dollars, not clicks.
02
Stack three aggregate proof numbers above the fold.
Hook's version: $240M revenue driven · 200+ contractors served · 175+ five-star reviews. Three numbers, no more. This is the trust down-payment that lets the visitor read the rest of the page with the burden of proof lifted. Skip it and every subsequent claim has to fight for itself.
You won't have $240M yet. Start with what's true — routes booked, revenue routed, five-star reviews. Even at 10 clients, "$4.2M routed to pool & waste operators, 87 new routes booked, 4.9★ average rating" is a legitimate strip. Update it monthly.
03
Publish pricing. At minimum, publish a floor.
Hook is the only agency in the sample that publishes real numbers, which is why they also harvest every "hvac marketing pricing" long-tail keyword in the category. Transparent pricing is a wedge, a trust signal, and an SEO magnet in one page. In a category where 90% of sites gate everything, this is a moat.
Publish at least a starting point — "Websites from $8k, monthly growth systems from $2,400/mo, revenue-share options available for the right fit." The buyer who can't afford you exits, the buyer who can arrives already qualified.
04
Productize into 2–3 named tiers.
Contractor Marketing Pros tiers by the client's revenue (Launch $1M–$3M, Rocket $3M–$5M, Platinum $5M+). This is genius because the buyer self-qualifies without you having to publish dollar prices. Comrade names the whole product (Revenue Engine) — same effect, different mechanism.
Name three tiers by what the buyer will be in a year, not what they are now. Something like Route · Region · Franchise. A pool-cleaning operator sees which one they aspire to and self-selects up.
05
Build one landing page per vertical, per service.
Hook operates /hvac-marketing/website-design/, /plumbing-marketing/website-design/, /roofing-marketing/website-design/. Same skeleton, different hero, different imagery, different testimonials. This is the SEO engine and the "you were built for me" trust move in one architectural decision.
Ship 4–6 vertical pages at launch: /pool-service-marketing/, /waste-management-marketing/, /hvac-marketing/, /junk-removal-marketing/. Even if two of them start identical, the URLs and titles are already earning SEO from day one.
06
Name a proprietary system — a product for the service.
Comrade sells the "Revenue Engine." Rival sells "systems." Naming turns you from freelancer-for-hire into product. It also gives the buyer a clean referral phrase — "they run this thing called the X" — which is worth more than any brochure.
You need a name. Not "The [Yourname] Method" (overused), but something concrete to the transformation. The Route Engine. The Full-Book System. The Steady Flow Framework. Whatever it is, it has to earn its name — a diagram, an ordered set of steps, a portal, something the buyer can point at.
07
Every case study title is the outcome, not the client name.
Hook's case study titles read as revenue events: "Turned $6K in Ad Spend Into $105K in Sold Work." Bottom-five case study titles read as filenames: "Coastal Roofing Case Study." The link text sells before the click. This is a copywriting decision, not a design decision.
Rewrite every case study title before you ship. "12 new commercial pool accounts in 90 days" beats "Aquatech Case Study" every time.
08
Harvest video testimonials aggressively.
Contractor Marketing Pros has 40+ video testimonials. That is their biggest compounding asset. Every closed engagement becomes a permanent piece of proof that never depreciates. Most agencies show 2–3 videos because they never asked past 3.
Build the ask into onboarding. At the 90-day milestone, a 60-second Loom-quality testimonial is a required deliverable — the client provides one before the next invoice. Ship a two-minute recording rig by year one.
09
Reframe the buyer's assumption in the hero.
Hook's "you don't have a lead problem, you have a presence problem" is worth more than any headline you could write from scratch. It rewrites the buyer's mental model to fit exactly what Hook sells. This is the highest-leverage copywriting move in the sample.
For your niche, the reframe might be "You don't have a marketing problem. You have a positioning problem." Or "Your competitors aren't better — they're just easier to find on Tuesday at 2pm." Test three, pick the one your target buyer nods at.
10
Show real humans — clients and your team, by name.
Hook names Maggie, Claudia, Vinny throughout reviews. KickCharge shows Antonelli replying to testimonials. Comrade shows Ivan's face. Every bottom-five site is faceless. Buyers in this category are choosing a partner, not a vendor — a face on the site is table stakes at the top and completely absent at the bottom.
Photo of you above the fold or in a "Meet [Your Name]" module by scroll two. Every account manager named on the About page with a photo, first name, and one specific thing they're known for.
11
Write a signature guide or short book. Position on it.
KickCharge (Antonelli, Branded Not "Blanded") and Rival (The HOME Advantage) both use a book as their founder-authority flywheel. It doesn't need to be Penguin-published — a 60-page PDF that's genuinely valuable functions the same way for a niche audience. It becomes your podcast pitch, your speaking pitch, your email nurture, your SEO magnet, and your credibility on cold calls.
Ship the first version in the first six months. "The Boring Business Growth Playbook" or "How High-Margin Service Businesses Fill Their Route in 90 Days." Give it away in exchange for an email. It compounds for a decade.
12
Own one recurring signature visual asset.
KickCharge shows a truck wrap on every scroll — that's the physical proof of their craft, a visual asset no competitor can fake. Hook shows the same office and the same team. You need the equivalent: a distinctive component, a signature photograph treatment, a recurring illustration, something buyers recognize by sight before they read a word.
For your niche the answer is probably a beautifully rendered "route map" or "territory map" — a stylized illustration of a service area with routes, accounts, and revenue overlaid. Ship a template. Every case study gets one. Every proposal opens with one. It becomes the shape of the agency in a client's mind.
